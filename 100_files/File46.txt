			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Abstentionism</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">

				</div>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div role="note" class="hatnote">This article is about the political strategy used in Ireland and Austria. For Christian theological positions relating to alcoholic beverages, see <a href="/wiki/Christianity_and_alcohol" class="mw-redirect" title="Christianity and alcohol">Christianity and alcohol</a>. For the US legal doctrine, see <a href="/wiki/Abstention_doctrine" title="Abstention doctrine">Abstention doctrine</a>.</div>
<p><b>Abstentionism</b> is standing for election to a <a href="/wiki/Deliberative_assembly" title="Deliberative assembly">deliberative assembly</a> while refusing to take up any seats won or otherwise participate in the assembly's business. Abstentionism differs from an <a href="/wiki/Election_boycott" title="Election boycott">election boycott</a> in that abstentionists participate in the election itself. Abstentionism has been used by <a href="/wiki/Irish_republicanism" title="Irish republicanism">Irish republican</a> political movements in the United Kingdom and Ireland since the early 19th century. It was also used by Hungarian and Czech nationalists in the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Council_(Austria)" title="Imperial Council (Austria)">Austrian Imperial Council</a> in the 1860s.<sup id="cite_ref-Agnew2004_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Agnew2004-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_Ireland">In Ireland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abstentionism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: In Ireland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Before_partition">Before partition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abstentionism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Before partition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After the <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_Union_1800" title="Acts of Union 1800">Act of Union 1800</a>, Ireland was represented at <a href="/wiki/Palace_of_Westminster" title="Palace of Westminster">Westminster</a> in the <a href="/wiki/British_House_of_Commons" class="mw-redirect" title="British House of Commons">House of Commons</a> of the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_of_Great_Britain_and_Ireland" title="United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland">United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland</a>. Repeal of the Act of Union was a goal of many <a href="/wiki/Irish_nationalism" title="Irish nationalism">Irish nationalists</a>.</p>
<p>In 1845 a motion was carried at the <a href="/wiki/Repeal_Association" title="Repeal Association">Repeal Association</a>'s committee for all Irish <a href="/wiki/Member_of_parliament" title="Member of parliament">members of parliament</a> (MPs) to withdraw from Westminster. It was proposed by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Osborne_Davis_(Irish_politician)" class="mw-redirect" title="Thomas Osborne Davis (Irish politician)">Thomas Osborne Davis</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Young_Ireland" title="Young Ireland">Young Ireland</a> movement. However, the committee felt that MPs already sitting could not withdraw without breaking the <a href="/wiki/Oath_of_office" title="Oath of office">oath of office</a> they had taken upon election.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Irish_Confederation" title="Irish Confederation">Irish Confederation</a>, which withdrew from the Repeal Association in 1847, resolved in favour of immediate abstention. However its founder, <a href="/wiki/William_Smith_O%27Brien" title="William Smith O'Brien">William Smith O'Brien</a>, continued to speak at Westminster.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> In 1848 <a href="/wiki/Charles_Gavan_Duffy" title="Charles Gavan Duffy">Charles Gavan Duffy</a> proposed that Irish MPs expelled from Westminster should sit in a separate Irish parliament.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other early abstentionist advocates included <a href="/wiki/George_Sigerson" title="George Sigerson">George Sigerson</a> in 1862, and <a href="/wiki/John_Dillon" title="John Dillon">John Dillon</a> in 1878, who envisaged abstentionist Irish MPs meeting in a separate Irish parliament.<sup id="cite_ref-lydon325_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lydon325-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>From the 1860s, <a href="/wiki/Irish_Republican_Brotherhood" title="Irish Republican Brotherhood">Irish Republican Brotherhood</a> leaders <a href="/wiki/Charles_Kickham" title="Charles Kickham">Charles Kickham</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_O%27Leary_(Fenian)" title="John O'Leary (Fenian)">John O'Leary</a> favoured abstentionism.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In 1869 <a href="/wiki/George_Henry_Moore_(politician)" title="George Henry Moore (politician)">G.H. Moore</a> suggested nominating imprisoned <a href="/wiki/Irish_republicanism" title="Irish republicanism">republicans</a> for election, knowing they were precluded as convicted <a href="/wiki/Felony" title="Felony">felons</a> from taking seats.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> On this basis, <a href="/wiki/Jeremiah_O%27Donovan_Rossa" title="Jeremiah O'Donovan Rossa">Jeremiah O'Donovan Rossa</a> (in 1870) and <a href="/wiki/John_Mitchel" title="John Mitchel">John Mitchel</a> (twice in 1875) were returned at by-elections in <a href="/wiki/Tipperary_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Tipperary (UK Parliament constituency)">Tipperary</a>; O'Donovan Rossa was in prison at his election, while Mitchel was in exile.</p>
<p>Kickham envisaged a "great national conference" calling on Irish MPs to withdraw from Westminster. A motion proposed by Charles Doran to that effect was passed at the convention of the <a href="/wiki/Home_Rule_League" title="Home Rule League">Home Rule League</a> (HRL).<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/John_Martin_(Ireland)" class="mw-redirect" title="John Martin (Ireland)">"Honest" John Martin</a>, "independent nationalist" MP for <a href="/wiki/Meath_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Meath (UK Parliament constituency)">Meath</a> 1871–75, spoke in Westminster only to raise nationalist protests, and refused to vote.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1874" title="United Kingdom general election, 1874">the 1874 election</a>, 59 HRL MPs were returned, including <a href="/wiki/John_O%27Connor_Power" title="John O'Connor Power">John O'Connor Power</a> in <a href="/wiki/Mayo_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Mayo (UK Parliament constituency)">Mayo</a>, who was a member of the IRB Supreme Council. He was to fall out with the IRB over allegations of misappropriating election funds,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and became progressively less radical. O'Connor Power believed that Westminster was the best platform to argue Ireland's case for self-government. Withdrawal from Parliament would be an abandonment of the Home Rule party to those who favoured conciliation rather than confrontation.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> By 1876 it was clear that the HRL would never be able to organise a national convention, and MPs elected with its endorsement would remain at Westminster.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> An alternative to abstentionism was <a href="/wiki/Obstructionism" title="Obstructionism">obstructionism</a>, including the use of <a href="/wiki/Filibuster" title="Filibuster">filibuster</a>. This was practised by the HRL and its successor, the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Parliamentary_Party" title="Irish Parliamentary Party">Irish Parliamentary Party</a> (IPP) under <a href="/wiki/Charles_Stuart_Parnell" class="mw-redirect" title="Charles Stuart Parnell">Charles Stuart Parnell</a> from the late 1870s.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Sinn_F.C3.A9in">Sinn Féin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abstentionism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Sinn Féin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Sinn_F%C3%A9in" title="History of Sinn Féin">History of Sinn Féin</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Arthur_Griffith" title="Arthur Griffith">Arthur Griffith</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/History_of_Sinn_F%C3%A9in" title="History of Sinn Féin">Sinn Féin Policy</a>", formulated 1905–07, called for Irish MPs to abstain from Westminster and sit in a parallel parliament in <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The first Sinn Féin abstentionist candidate was <a href="/wiki/Charles_Dolan_(Irish_politician)" class="mw-redirect" title="Charles Dolan (Irish politician)">Charles Dolan</a> in 1908. Having sat as MP for <a href="/wiki/North_Leitrim_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="North Leitrim (UK Parliament constituency)">North Leitrim</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Parliamentary_Party" title="Irish Parliamentary Party">Irish Parliamentary Party</a>, he resigned after joining Sinn Féin, and lost the ensuing by-election.<sup id="cite_ref-lydon325_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lydon325-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Abstentionism was opposed by most nationalists, especially after the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_January_1910" title="United Kingdom general election, January 1910">January 1910 general election</a> when the IPP held the <a href="/wiki/Balance_of_power_(parliament)" title="Balance of power (parliament)">balance of power</a> at Westminster and secured passage of the <a href="/wiki/Third_Home_Rule_Bill" class="mw-redirect" title="Third Home Rule Bill">Third Home Rule Bill</a> from <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Government_1905-1915" class="mw-redirect" title="Liberal Government 1905-1915">the Liberal government</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> This changed after the <a href="/wiki/Easter_Rising" title="Easter Rising">1916 Rising</a>, and the IPP itself withdrew from Westminster in April 1918 to protest against the <a href="/wiki/Irish_conscription_crisis" class="mw-redirect" title="Irish conscription crisis">extension of conscription to Ireland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first abstentionist MP elected was Count <a href="/wiki/George_Noble_Plunkett" title="George Noble Plunkett">George Noble Plunkett</a> after the <a href="/wiki/North_Roscommon_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="North Roscommon (UK Parliament constituency)">North Roscommon</a> by-election of 3 February 1917.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Plunkett did not categorically state his abstentionism until after his victory.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Plunkett's Liberty League, Griffith's monarchist Sinn Féin, and the northern Irish Nation League merged later that year into a reconstituted <a href="/wiki/Sinn_F%C3%A9in" title="Sinn Féin">Sinn Féin</a>, agreeing after contentious disputation that abstentionism was a principle rather than merely a tactic.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> In 1919, Sinn Féin MPs <a href="/wiki/Irish_general_election,_1918" title="Irish general election, 1918">elected to Westminster in 1918</a> refused to take their seats there and instead constituted themselves in Dublin as the TDs (<span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga"><i><a href="/wiki/Teachta_D%C3%A1la" title="Teachta Dála">Teachtaí Dála</a></i></span>) of the <a href="/wiki/First_D%C3%A1il" title="First Dáil">first <i>Dáil</i></a>, which was claimed to be the legitimate parliament of the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Republic" title="Irish Republic">Irish Republic</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Sinn Féin was unsure whether to boycott the <a href="/wiki/Irish_general_election,_1921" class="mw-redirect" title="Irish general election, 1921">1921 elections</a> to the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Commons_of_Northern_Ireland" title="House of Commons of Northern Ireland">House of Commons of Northern Ireland</a> and <a href="/wiki/House_of_Commons_of_Southern_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="House of Commons of Southern Ireland">House of Commons of Southern Ireland</a> set up by the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Ireland_Act_1920" title="Government of Ireland Act 1920">Government of Ireland Act 1920</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-feeney130x1_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-feeney130x1-21">[21]</a></sup> It decided to contest the Northern election for tactical reasons and the Southern one for consistency, with its returned MPs becoming the TDs of the <a href="/wiki/Second_D%C3%A1il" title="Second Dáil">Second Dáil</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-feeney130x1_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-feeney130x1-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Irish_republican_legitimatism" class="mw-redirect" title="Irish republican legitimatism">One strand within Republicanism</a>, in remaining loyal to this pre-<a href="/wiki/Partition_of_Ireland" title="Partition of Ireland">Partition</a> Irish Republic, denies the <a href="/wiki/Legitimacy_(political_science)" class="mw-redirect" title="Legitimacy (political science)">legitimacy</a> of both the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Other parties reached accommodation with the southern state but not Northern Ireland. Some groups have boycotted elections within either jurisdiction; others have been abstentionist; others abstained from some bodies but not others. Abstentionism has often been a divisive issue within Republicanism.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_the_southern_state">In the southern state</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abstentionism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: In the southern state">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The 1921 <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Irish_Treaty" title="Anglo-Irish Treaty">Anglo-Irish Treaty</a> established the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Free_State" title="Irish Free State">Irish Free State</a>, with an opt-out for <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> and requiring an <a href="/wiki/Oath_of_Allegiance_(Ireland)" title="Oath of Allegiance (Ireland)">Oath of Allegiance</a> for <a href="/wiki/Oireachtas_(Irish_Free_State)" class="mw-redirect" title="Oireachtas (Irish Free State)">Free State legislators</a>. The Treaty split Sinn Féin, mainly over the Oath rather than "<a href="/wiki/Partition_of_Ireland" title="Partition of Ireland">Partition</a>", and caused the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Civil_War" title="Irish Civil War">Irish Civil War</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Irish_general_election,_1922" title="Irish general election, 1922">June 1922 election</a> featured a "Sinn Féin panel" of pro- and anti-Treaty candidates, but the resulting <a href="/wiki/Third_D%C3%A1il" title="Third Dáil">Third Dáil</a> was boycotted by the anti-Treaty TDs. These refounded Sinn Féin in 1923 and based their continued abstention from the <a href="/wiki/Free_State_D%C3%A1il" class="mw-redirect" title="Free State Dáil">Free State Dáil</a> on Partition.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Fianna_F%C3%A1il" title="Fianna Fáil">Fianna Fáil</a> split from Sinn Féin in 1926 and abandoned abstentionism in the Free State in 1927.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> In 1955, Sinn Féin contested <a href="/wiki/Local_government_in_the_Republic_of_Ireland" title="Local government in the Republic of Ireland">local elections in the Republic of Ireland</a> and took its seats.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In 1970, at its <a href="/wiki/Ard_Fheis" class="mw-redirect" title="Ard Fheis">Ard Fheis</a> (annual conference), Sinn Féin split again on the issue of whether or not to reverse its long-standing policy of refusing to take seats in <a href="/wiki/D%C3%A1il_%C3%89ireann" title="Dáil Éireann">Dáil Éireann</a>. The split created two parties calling themselves "Sinn Féin". The anti-abstentionist party was known as "Official" Sinn Féin. It changed its name to "Sinn Féin the Workers Party" (SFWP) and won a seat in the Dáil in <a href="/wiki/Irish_general_election,_1981" title="Irish general election, 1981">the general election of 1981</a>, which it took. The following year it dropped "Sinn Féin" from its name to become "<a href="/wiki/Workers%27_Party_of_Ireland" title="Workers' Party of Ireland">The Workers' Party</a>". The abstentionist party was initially referred to as "Provisional" Sinn Féin, but after 1982 it was known simply as "Sinn Féin"; it continued to abstain from taking seats won in all institutions.</p>
<p>In 1986 Sinn Féin split, as in 1970, over whether to take seats in Dáil Éireann. The larger group led by <a href="/wiki/Gerry_Adams" title="Gerry Adams">Gerry Adams</a> abandoned abstentionism, while <a href="/wiki/Republican_Sinn_F%C3%A9in" title="Republican Sinn Féin">Republican Sinn Féin</a> (RSF), led by <a href="/wiki/Ruair%C3%AD_%C3%93_Br%C3%A1daigh" title="Ruairí Ó Brádaigh">Ruairí Ó Brádaigh</a> retained it. Sinn Féin's first sitting <a href="/wiki/Teachta_D%C3%A1la" title="Teachta Dála">Teachta Dála</a> was <a href="/wiki/Caoimhgh%C3%ADn_%C3%93_Caol%C3%A1in" title="Caoimhghín Ó Caoláin">Caoimhghín Ó Caoláin</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cavan%E2%80%93Monaghan_(D%C3%A1il_%C3%89ireann_constituency)" title="Cavan–Monaghan (Dáil Éireann constituency)">Cavan–Monaghan</a> in <a href="/wiki/Irish_general_election,_1997" title="Irish general election, 1997">1997</a>.</p>
<p>RSF has retained the policy of abstentionism from both Dáil Éireann and the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Assembly" title="Northern Ireland Assembly">Northern Ireland Assembly</a>. RSF has not in fact contested elections for Dáil Éireann or Westminster.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_Northern_Ireland">In Northern Ireland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abstentionism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: In Northern Ireland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After Partition, most non-abstentionist parties in the southern state did not organise at all in Northern Ireland. In early 1922 the <a href="/wiki/Provisional_Government_of_the_Irish_Free_State" class="mw-redirect" title="Provisional Government of the Irish Free State">Provisional Government of the Irish Free State</a> was seen as representing the interests of nationalists in Northern Ireland and had a policy of not recognising the Northern Irish government. Catholic archbishop <a href="/wiki/Joseph_MacRory" title="Joseph MacRory">Joseph MacRory</a> indicated to the Provisional Government that <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Devlin" title="Joseph Devlin">Joe Devlin</a> and his party members wanted to enter the new <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Northern_Ireland" title="Parliament of Northern Ireland">Parliament of Northern Ireland</a>, and was worried that the policy of non-recognition would result in Northern Irish nationalists having to "fight alone", but his advice was ignored.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Nationalist_Party_(Northern_Ireland)" title="Nationalist Party (Northern Ireland)">Nationalist Party</a> did not take their seats during the <a href="/wiki/MPs_elected_in_the_Northern_Ireland_general_election,_1921" class="mw-redirect" title="MPs elected in the Northern Ireland general election, 1921">first Stormont parliament (1921–25)</a>. Despite forming the second-largest <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_party" class="mw-redirect" title="Parliamentary party">parliamentary party</a>, they did not accept the role of <a href="/wiki/Opposition_(parliamentary)" title="Opposition (parliamentary)">Opposition</a> for a further forty years. They did so on 2 February 1965 but withdrew from opposition again in October 1968, two weeks after police batonned demonstrators at a <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Civil_Rights_Association#Derry_march" title="Northern Ireland Civil Rights Association">civil rights march</a> in Derry on 5 October 1968.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Cahir_Healy" title="Cahir Healy">Cahir Healy</a> was elected to both the Stormont and Westminster parliaments under a variety of nationalist labels between the 1920s and the 1960s. He was abstentionist in Stormont until 1927 and at Westminster from 1950 to 1952.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> In the 1930s, Healy led the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Union_Association" class="mw-redirect" title="Irish Union Association">Irish Union Association</a>, which supported his policy of intermittent tactical abstentionism, whereas the otherwise-similar <a href="/wiki/Northern_Council_for_Unity" title="Northern Council for Unity">Northern Council for Unity</a> regarded abstentionism as a principle.<sup id="cite_ref-Norton2007_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Norton2007-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>From 1953, Stormont candidates were required to take the <a href="/wiki/Oath_of_Allegiance_(United_Kingdom)" title="Oath of Allegiance (United Kingdom)">British oath of allegiance</a> before standing, precluding Sinn Féin from doing so.<sup id="cite_ref-feeney199_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-feeney199-31">[31]</a></sup> This did not apply at Westminster elections, where Sinn Féin often gave non-Sinn Féin abstentionist nationalists a free run to avoid <a href="/wiki/Vote_splitting" title="Vote splitting">splitting</a> the nationalist vote, but conversely fielded a <a href="/wiki/Spoiler_effect" title="Spoiler effect">spoiler</a> candidate against non-abstentionist nationalists.<sup id="cite_ref-feeney199_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-feeney199-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_and_Labour_Party" title="Social Democratic and Labour Party">Social Democratic and Labour Party</a> (SDLP) became the Opposition on its formation on 21 August 1970 but that party withdrew from Stormont in July 1971. The SDLP participated in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Assembly,_1973" class="mw-redirect" title="Northern Ireland Assembly, 1973">the assembly</a> set up for the <a href="/wiki/Sunningdale_Agreement" title="Sunningdale Agreement">Sunningdale Agreement</a>, and in the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Constitutional_Convention" title="Northern Ireland Constitutional Convention">Constitutional Convention</a>. It originally intended to <a href="/wiki/Election_boycott" title="Election boycott">boycott the election</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Assembly,_1982" class="mw-redirect" title="Northern Ireland Assembly, 1982">1982 Assembly</a>, but adopted abstentionism to avoid giving a free run to Sinn Féin.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Brian Feeney suggests that Sinn Féin's "active abstention", where those elected acted as local spokespeople in the media, was more effective than the SDLP's policy of sending its representatives instead to the <a href="/wiki/New_Ireland_Forum" title="New Ireland Forum">New Ireland Forum</a> in Dublin.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> The SDLP's participation in the 1996–98 <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Forum" title="Northern Ireland Forum">Northern Ireland Forum</a> was intermittent.</p>
<p>(Provisional) Sinn Féin adopted the "<a href="/wiki/Armalite_and_ballot_box_strategy" title="Armalite and ballot box strategy">armalite and ballot box strategy</a>" in 1981, and first contested modern elections in Northern Ireland with the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Assembly_election,_1982" title="Northern Ireland Assembly election, 1982">1982 Assembly elections</a>, from which they abstained. The 1983 <a href="/wiki/Ardfheis" title="Ardfheis">ardfheis</a> resolved to take seats in the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament" title="European Parliament">European Parliament</a>, as the 1985 ardfheis did for <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_local_elections,_1985" title="Northern Ireland local elections, 1985">that year's local elections</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Sinn Féin abstained from the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Forum" title="Northern Ireland Forum">Northern Ireland Forum</a>.</p>
<p>Since the establishment of the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Assembly" title="Northern Ireland Assembly">Northern Ireland Assembly</a> under the 1998 <a href="/wiki/Good_Friday_Agreement" title="Good Friday Agreement">Good Friday Agreement</a>, both the SDLP and Sinn Féin have taken their seats in that body. SDLP MPs have consistently taken their seats in Westminster, in contrast to Sinn Féin MPs who refuse to take their seats there, as they refuse to recognise that body's right to legislate for any part of Ireland.</p>
<p>Fianna Fáil's sole Stormont election came <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_general_election,_1933" title="Northern Ireland general election, 1933">in 1933</a>, when its leader <a href="/wiki/%C3%89amon_de_Valera" title="Éamon de Valera">Éamon de Valera</a> agreed to stand as an abstentionist for <a href="/wiki/South_Down_(Northern_Ireland_Parliament_constituency)" title="South Down (Northern Ireland Parliament constituency)">South Down</a>, where he had been a Sinn Féin MP in the 1920s.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Kelly2013_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kelly2013-36">[36]</a></sup> Fianna Fáil registered as a political party within Northern Ireland in 2007. In 2014 its leader <a href="/wiki/M%C3%ADche%C3%A1l_Martin" class="mw-redirect" title="Mícheál Martin">Mícheál Martin</a> announced it would contest elections from 2019.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> It has not made clear whether it will contest elections to Westminster.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Republican_Sinn_F%C3%A9in" title="Republican Sinn Féin">Republican Sinn Féin</a> continue their long standing policy of abstentionism. It is not a registered party in Northern Ireland, but members have contested the Assembly elections as <a href="/wiki/Independent_(politics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Independent (politics)">independents</a>. When <a href="/wiki/Saoradh" title="Saoradh">Saoradh</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Dissident_republican" title="Dissident republican">dissident republican</a> party, was established in 2016, it had not decided whether to contest elections, but said it would in any case abstain from taking up any seats won in Stormont, Westminster or Leinster House.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hungary">Hungary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abstentionism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Hungary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Diet_of_Hungary" title="Diet of Hungary">Diet of Hungary</a> was suppressed by the <a href="/wiki/Habsburg_Empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Habsburg Empire">Habsburg Empire</a> after the <a href="/wiki/Hungarian_Revolution_of_1848" title="Hungarian Revolution of 1848">1848 Revolution</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Agnew2004_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Agnew2004-1">[1]</a></sup> Austria's 1861 <a href="/wiki/February_Patent" title="February Patent">February Patent</a> reserved places for Hungary in the indirectly-elected <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Council_(Austria)" title="Imperial Council (Austria)">Imperial Council</a>, but the Hungarians did not send representatives, arguing the Council was usurping authority properly belonging to the Diet.<sup id="cite_ref-Agnew2004_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Agnew2004-1">[1]</a></sup> Emulating the Hungarians, the <a href="/wiki/Czechs" title="Czechs">Czech</a> delegates for <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Bohemia" title="Kingdom of Bohemia">Bohemia</a> withdrew in 1863, and those from <a href="/wiki/Margraviate_of_Moravia" title="Margraviate of Moravia">Moravia</a> in 1864.<sup id="cite_ref-Agnew2004_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Agnew2004-1">[1]</a></sup> Hungarian demands were met by the <a href="/wiki/Austro-Hungarian_Compromise_of_1867" title="Austro-Hungarian Compromise of 1867">Compromise of 1867</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Agnew2004_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Agnew2004-1">[1]</a></sup> In 1904, <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Griffith" title="Arthur Griffith">Arthur Griffith</a> published <i><a href="/wiki/The_Resurrection_of_Hungary" title="The Resurrection of Hungary">The Resurrection of Hungary</a></i> arguing for a British-Irish <a href="/wiki/Dual_monarchy" title="Dual monarchy">dual monarchy</a> on the 1867 model. Griffith's subsequent "<i>Sinn Féin</i> policy" developed this model.</p>

<p><b>Aikido</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">Japanese</a>: <span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">合気道</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <a href="/wiki/Hepburn_romanization" title="Hepburn romanization">Hepburn</a>: <i>aikidō</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Japanese" title="Help:IPA for Japanese">[aikiꜜdoː]</a></span> is a <a href="/wiki/Gendai_bud%C5%8D" title="Gendai budō">modern</a> <a href="/wiki/Japanese_martial_art" class="mw-redirect" title="Japanese martial art">Japanese martial art</a> developed by <a href="/wiki/Morihei_Ueshiba" title="Morihei Ueshiba">Morihei Ueshiba</a> as a synthesis of his martial studies, philosophy, and religious beliefs. Aikido is often translated as "the way of unifying (with) <a href="/wiki/Qi" title="Qi">life energy</a>"<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> or as "the way of harmonious spirit".<sup id="cite_ref-ADS_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ADS-2">[2]</a></sup> Ueshiba's goal was to create an art that practitioners could use to defend themselves while also protecting their attacker from injury.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Aikido techniques consist of <a href="/wiki/Irimi" title="Irimi">entering</a> and turning movements that redirect the <a href="/wiki/Momentum" title="Momentum">momentum</a> of an opponent's attack, and a <a href="/wiki/Throw_(grappling)" title="Throw (grappling)">throw</a> or <a href="/wiki/Joint_lock" title="Joint lock">joint lock</a> that terminates the technique.<sup id="cite_ref-Aikido_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Aikido-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Aikido derives mainly from the martial art of <a href="/wiki/Dait%C5%8D-ry%C5%AB_Aiki-j%C5%ABjutsu" title="Daitō-ryū Aiki-jūjutsu">Daitō-ryū Aiki-jūjutsu</a>, but began to diverge from it in the late 1920s, partly due to Ueshiba's involvement with the <a href="/wiki/Oomoto" title="Oomoto">Ōmoto-kyō</a> religion. Ueshiba's early students' documents bear the term <i>aiki-jūjutsu</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Pranin-Aikijujutsu_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pranin-Aikijujutsu-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ueshiba's senior students have different approaches to aikido, depending partly on when they studied with him. Today aikido is found all over the world in a number of styles, with broad ranges of interpretation and emphasis. However, they all share techniques formulated by Ueshiba and most have concern for the well-being of the attacker.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology_and_basic_philosophy">Etymology and basic philosophy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aikido&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology and basic philosophy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:82px;"><a href="/wiki/File:%E5%90%88%E6%B0%A3%E9%81%93.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/%E5%90%88%E6%B0%A3%E9%81%93.svg/80px-%E5%90%88%E6%B0%A3%E9%81%93.svg.png" width="80" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/%E5%90%88%E6%B0%A3%E9%81%93.svg/120px-%E5%90%88%E6%B0%A3%E9%81%93.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/%E5%90%88%E6%B0%A3%E9%81%93.svg/160px-%E5%90%88%E6%B0%A3%E9%81%93.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="100" data-file-height="366" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:%E5%90%88%E6%B0%A3%E9%81%93.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
"Aikidō" written with "<a href="/wiki/Qi" title="Qi">ki</a>" in its <a href="/wiki/Ky%C5%ABjitai" title="Kyūjitai">old character form</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The word "aikido" is formed of three <a href="/wiki/Kanji" title="Kanji">kanji</a>:</p>
<ul>
<li><span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/en:%E5%90%88" class="extiw" title="wikt:en:合">合</a></span>&#160;– <i>ai</i>&#160;– joining, unifying, combining, fitting</li>
<li><span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/en:%E6%B0%97" class="extiw" title="wikt:en:気">気</a></span>&#160;– <i>ki</i>&#160;– spirit, energy, mood, morale</li>
<li><span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/en:%E9%81%93" class="extiw" title="wikt:en:道">道</a></span>&#160;– <i>dō</i>&#160;– way, path</li>
</ul>
<p>The term <i><a href="/wiki/Aiki_(martial_arts_principle)" title="Aiki (martial arts principle)">aiki</a></i> does not readily appear in the Japanese language outside the scope of <a href="/wiki/Bud%C5%8D" title="Budō">budō</a>. This has led to many possible interpretations of the word. <span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">合</span> is mainly used in compounds to mean 'combine, unite, join together, meet', examples being <span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">合同</span> (combined/united), <span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">合成</span> (composition), <span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">結合</span> (unite/combine/join together), <span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">連合</span> (union/alliance/association), <span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">統合</span> (combine/unify), and <span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">合意</span> (mutual agreement). There is an idea of <a href="/wiki/Norm_of_reciprocity" title="Norm of reciprocity">reciprocity</a>, <span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">知り合う</span> (to get to know one another), <span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">話し合い</span> (talk/discussion/negotiation), and <span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">待ち合わせる</span> (meet by appointment).</p>
<p><span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">気</span> is often used to describe a feeling, as in <span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">X気がする</span> ('I feel X', as in terms of thinking but with less cognitive reasoning), and <span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">気持ち</span> (feeling/sensation); it is used to mean energy or force, as in <span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">電気</span> (electricity) and <span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">磁気</span> (magnetism); it can also refer to qualities or aspects of people or things, as in <span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">気質</span> (spirit/trait/temperament).</p>
<p>The term <span title="Japanese transliteration" class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none">dō</span> is also found in martial arts such as <a href="/wiki/Judo" title="Judo">judo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kendo" title="Kendo">kendo</a>, and in various non-martial arts, such as <a href="/wiki/Japanese_calligraphy" title="Japanese calligraphy">Japanese calligraphy</a> (<span title="Japanese transliteration" class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none">shodō</span>), <a href="/wiki/Ikebana" title="Ikebana">flower arranging</a> (<span title="Japanese transliteration" class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none">kadō</span>) and <a href="/wiki/Japanese_tea_ceremony" title="Japanese tea ceremony">tea ceremony</a> (<span title="Japanese transliteration" class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none">chadō or sadō</span>).</p>
<p>Therefore, from a purely literal interpretation, aikido is the "Way of combining forces", in that the term <i>aiki</i> refers to the martial arts principle or tactic of blending with an attacker's movements for the purpose of controlling their actions with minimal effort.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> One applies <span title="Japanese transliteration" class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none">aiki</span> by understanding the rhythm and intent of the attacker to find the optimal position and timing to apply a counter-technique.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aikido&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:102px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Morihei_Ueshiba_1939.jpg" class="image"><img alt="A middle-aged, mustachioed man in a kimono" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Morihei_Ueshiba_1939.jpg/100px-Morihei_Ueshiba_1939.jpg" width="100" height="144" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Morihei_Ueshiba_1939.jpg/150px-Morihei_Ueshiba_1939.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Morihei_Ueshiba_1939.jpg/200px-Morihei_Ueshiba_1939.jpg 2x" data-file-width="280" data-file-height="404" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Morihei_Ueshiba_1939.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Ueshiba in Tokyo in 1939</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Aikido was created by Morihei Ueshiba (<span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">植芝 盛平</span> <span title="Japanese transliteration" class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none"><i><a href="/wiki/Ueshiba_Morihei" class="mw-redirect" title="Ueshiba Morihei">Ueshiba Morihei</a></i></span>, 14 December 1883&#160;– 26 April 1969), referred to by some aikido practitioners as <span title="Japanese transliteration" class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none"><i>Ōsensei</i></span> (<i>Great Teacher</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The term <i>aikido</i> was coined in the twentieth century.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Ueshiba envisioned aikido not only as the synthesis of his martial training, but as an expression of his personal philosophy of universal peace and reconciliation. During Ueshiba's lifetime and continuing today, aikido has evolved from the <i>aiki</i> that Ueshiba studied into a variety of expressions by martial artists throughout the world.<sup id="cite_ref-Aikido_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Aikido-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Initial_development">Initial development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aikido&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Initial development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Takeda_Sokaku.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Takeda_Sokaku.jpg/220px-Takeda_Sokaku.jpg" width="220" height="324" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b6/Takeda_Sokaku.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="278" data-file-height="409" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Takeda_Sokaku.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Takeda Sōkaku</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Ueshiba developed aikido primarily during the late 1920s through the 1930s through the synthesis of the older martial arts that he had studied.<sup id="cite_ref-AWH_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AWH-10">[10]</a></sup> The core martial art from which aikido derives is <a href="/wiki/Daito-ryu_aiki-jujutsu" class="mw-redirect" title="Daito-ryu aiki-jujutsu">Daitō-ryū aiki-jūjutsu</a>, which Ueshiba studied directly with <a href="/wiki/Takeda_S%C5%8Dkaku" title="Takeda Sōkaku">Takeda Sōkaku</a>, the reviver of that art. Additionally, Ueshiba is known to have studied <a href="/wiki/Tenjin_Shinyo_Ryu" class="mw-redirect" title="Tenjin Shinyo Ryu">Tenjin Shin'yō-ryū</a> with Tozawa Tokusaburō in <a href="/wiki/Tokyo" title="Tokyo">Tokyo</a> in 1901, <a href="/wiki/Yagy%C5%AB_Shingan-ry%C5%AB" title="Yagyū Shingan-ryū">Gotōha Yagyū Shingan-ryū</a> under Nakai Masakatsu in <a href="/wiki/Sakai,_Osaka" title="Sakai, Osaka">Sakai</a> from 1903 to 1908, and <a href="/wiki/Judo" title="Judo">judo</a> with Kiyoichi Takagi (<span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">高木 喜代市</span> <span title="Japanese transliteration" class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none"><i>Takagi Kiyoichi</i></span>, 1894–1972) in <a href="/wiki/Tanabe" class="mw-redirect" title="Tanabe">Tanabe</a> in 1911.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The art of Daitō-ryū is the primary technical influence on aikido. Along with empty-handed throwing and joint-locking techniques, Ueshiba incorporated training movements with weapons, such as those for the <a href="/wiki/Spear" title="Spear">spear</a> (<span title="Japanese transliteration" class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none"><i><a href="/wiki/Yari" title="Yari">yari</a></i></span>), short <a href="/wiki/Stick_fighting" class="mw-redirect" title="Stick fighting">staff</a> (<span title="Japanese transliteration" class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none"><i><a href="/wiki/J%C5%8D" title="Jō">jō</a></i></span>), and perhaps the <a href="/wiki/Bayonet" title="Bayonet">bayonet</a> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">銃剣</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>jūken</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>. However, aikido derives much of its technical structure from the art of swordsmanship (<span title="Japanese transliteration" class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none"><i><a href="/wiki/Kenjutsu" title="Kenjutsu">kenjutsu</a></i></span>).<sup id="cite_ref-ADS_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ADS-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-TSoA_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TSoA-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ueshiba moved to <a href="/wiki/Hokkaid%C5%8D" class="mw-redirect" title="Hokkaidō">Hokkaidō</a> in 1912, and began studying under Takeda Sokaku in 1915. His official association with Daitō-ryū continued until 1937.<sup id="cite_ref-AWH_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AWH-10">[10]</a></sup> However, during the latter part of that period, Ueshiba had already begun to distance himself from Takeda and the Daitō-ryū. At that time Ueshiba was referring to his martial art as <b>"Aiki Budō"</b>. It is unclear exactly when Ueshiba began using the name "aikido", but it became the official name of the art in 1942 when the Greater Japan Martial Virtue Society (<span title="Japanese transliteration" class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none"><i><a href="/wiki/Dai_Nippon_Butoku_Kai" title="Dai Nippon Butoku Kai">Dai Nippon Butoku Kai</a></i></span>) was engaged in a government sponsored reorganization and centralization of Japanese martial arts.<sup id="cite_ref-Aikido_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Aikido-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Religious_influences">Religious influences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aikido&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Religious influences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Onisaburo_Deguchi_2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Onisaburo_Deguchi_2.jpg/220px-Onisaburo_Deguchi_2.jpg" width="220" height="313" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Onisaburo_Deguchi_2.jpg/330px-Onisaburo_Deguchi_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/25/Onisaburo_Deguchi_2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="353" data-file-height="502" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Onisaburo_Deguchi_2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Onisaburo Deguchi</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>After Ueshiba left Hokkaidō in 1919, he met and was profoundly influenced by <a href="/wiki/Onisaburo_Deguchi" title="Onisaburo Deguchi">Onisaburo Deguchi</a>, the spiritual leader of the <a href="/wiki/Oomoto" title="Oomoto">Ōmoto-kyō</a> religion (a neo-<a href="/wiki/Shinto" title="Shinto">Shinto</a> movement) in <a href="/wiki/Ayabe" class="mw-redirect" title="Ayabe">Ayabe</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> One of the primary features of Ōmoto-kyō is its emphasis on the attainment of <a href="/wiki/Utopia" title="Utopia">utopia</a> during one's life. This was a great influence on Ueshiba's martial arts philosophy of extending love and compassion especially to those who seek to harm others. Aikido demonstrates this philosophy in its emphasis on mastering martial arts so that one may receive an attack and harmlessly redirect it. In an ideal resolution, not only is the receiver unharmed, but so is the attacker.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>In addition to the effect on his spiritual growth, the connection with Deguchi gave Ueshiba entry to elite political and military circles as a martial artist. As a result of this exposure, he was able to attract not only financial backing but also gifted students. Several of these students would found their own styles of aikido.<sup id="cite_ref-Styles_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Styles-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="International_dissemination">International dissemination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aikido&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: International dissemination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Aikido was first brought to the rest of the world in 1951 by <a href="/wiki/Minoru_Mochizuki" title="Minoru Mochizuki">Minoru Mochizuki</a> with a visit to <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> where he introduced aikido techniques to judo students.<sup id="cite_ref-Mochizuki_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mochizuki-16">[16]</a></sup> He was followed by <a href="/wiki/Tadashi_Abe" title="Tadashi Abe">Tadashi Abe</a> in 1952, who came as the official <a href="/wiki/Aikikai_Hombu" class="mw-redirect" title="Aikikai Hombu">Aikikai Hombu</a> representative, remaining in France for seven years. <a href="/wiki/Kenji_Tomiki" title="Kenji Tomiki">Kenji Tomiki</a> toured with a delegation of various martial arts through 15 continental states of the United States in 1953.<sup id="cite_ref-Styles_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Styles-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Later that year, <a href="/wiki/Koichi_Tohei" title="Koichi Tohei">Koichi Tohei</a> was sent by Aikikai Hombu to <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a> for a full year, where he set up several <a href="/wiki/Dojo" title="Dojo">dojo</a>. This trip was followed by several further visits and is considered the formal introduction of aikido to the United States. The United Kingdom followed in 1955; Italy in 1964 by <a href="/wiki/Hiroshi_Tada" title="Hiroshi Tada">Hiroshi Tada</a>; and Germany in 1965 by <a href="/wiki/Katsuaki_Asai" title="Katsuaki Asai">Katsuaki Asai</a>. Designated "Official Delegate for Europe and Africa" by Morihei Ueshiba, <a href="/wiki/Masamichi_Noro" title="Masamichi Noro">Masamichi Noro</a> arrived in France in September 1961. <a href="/wiki/Seiichi_Sugano" title="Seiichi Sugano">Seiichi Sugano</a> was appointed to introduce aikido to Australia in 1965. Today there are aikido dojo throughout the world.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Proliferation_of_independent_organizations">Proliferation of independent organizations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aikido&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Proliferation of independent organizations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Aikido_styles" title="Aikido styles">Aikido styles</a></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_aikidoka" title="List of aikidoka">List of aikidōka</a></div>
<p>The largest aikido organization is the <a href="/wiki/Aikikai_Foundation" class="mw-redirect" title="Aikikai Foundation">Aikikai Foundation</a>, which remains under the control of the Ueshiba family. However, aikido has many styles, mostly formed by Morihei Ueshiba's major students.<sup id="cite_ref-Styles_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Styles-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The earliest independent styles to emerge were <a href="/wiki/Yoseikan_Aikido" title="Yoseikan Aikido">Yoseikan Aikido</a>, begun by <a href="/wiki/Minoru_Mochizuki" title="Minoru Mochizuki">Minoru Mochizuki</a> in 1931,<sup id="cite_ref-Mochizuki_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mochizuki-16">[16]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Yoshinkan" title="Yoshinkan">Yoshinkan</a> Aikido, founded by <a href="/wiki/Gozo_Shioda" title="Gozo Shioda">Gozo Shioda</a> in 1955,<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Shodokan_Aikido" title="Shodokan Aikido">Shodokan Aikido</a>, founded by <a href="/wiki/Kenji_Tomiki" title="Kenji Tomiki">Kenji Tomiki</a> in 1967.<sup id="cite_ref-ACE_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ACE-19">[19]</a></sup> The emergence of these styles pre-dated Ueshiba's death and did not cause any major upheavals when they were formalized. Shodokan Aikido, however, was controversial, since it introduced a unique rule-based competition that some felt was contrary to the spirit of aikido.<sup id="cite_ref-Styles_15-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Styles-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>After Ueshiba's death in 1969, two more major styles emerged. Significant controversy arose with the departure of the <a href="/wiki/Aikikai_Hombu_Dojo" class="mw-redirect" title="Aikikai Hombu Dojo">Aikikai Hombu Dojo</a>'s chief instructor <a href="/wiki/Koichi_Tohei" title="Koichi Tohei">Koichi Tohei</a>, in 1974. Tohei left as a result of a disagreement with the son of the founder, <a href="/wiki/Kisshomaru_Ueshiba" title="Kisshomaru Ueshiba">Kisshomaru Ueshiba</a>, who at that time headed the Aikikai Foundation. The disagreement was over the proper role of <i>ki</i> development in regular aikido training. After Tohei left, he formed his own style, called <a href="/wiki/Shin_Shin_Toitsu_Aikido" class="mw-redirect" title="Shin Shin Toitsu Aikido">Shin Shin Toitsu Aikido</a>, and the organization that governs it, the <a href="/wiki/Ki_Society" title="Ki Society">Ki Society</a> (<i>Ki no Kenkyūkai</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>A final major style evolved from Ueshiba's retirement in <a href="/wiki/Iwama,_Ibaraki" title="Iwama, Ibaraki">Iwama, Ibaraki</a> and the teaching methodology of long term student <a href="/wiki/Morihiro_Saito" title="Morihiro Saito">Morihiro Saito</a>. It is unofficially referred to as the "<a href="/wiki/Iwama_style" title="Iwama style">Iwama style</a>", and at one point a number of its followers formed a loose network of schools they called <a href="/wiki/Iwama_Ryu" class="mw-redirect" title="Iwama Ryu">Iwama Ryu</a>. Although Iwama style practitioners remained part of the Aikikai until Saito's death in 2002, followers of Saito subsequently split into two groups. One remained with the Aikikai and the other formed the independent <a href="/wiki/Shinshin_Aikishuren_Kai" class="mw-redirect" title="Shinshin Aikishuren Kai">Shinshin Aikishuren Kai</a> in 2004 around Saito's son <a href="/wiki/Hitohiro_Saito" title="Hitohiro Saito">Hitohiro Saito</a>.</p>
<p>Today, the major styles of aikido are each run by a separate governing organization, have their own headquarters <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">本部道場</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>honbu dōjō</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> in Japan, and have an international breadth.<sup id="cite_ref-Styles_15-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Styles-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ki">Ki</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aikido&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Ki">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:102px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ki_obsolete.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Ki_obsolete.svg/100px-Ki_obsolete.svg.png" width="100" height="100" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Ki_obsolete.svg/150px-Ki_obsolete.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Ki_obsolete.svg/200px-Ki_obsolete.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="320" data-file-height="320" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ki_obsolete.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
This was the <a href="/wiki/Kanji" title="Kanji">kanji</a> for <i>ki</i> until 1946, when <a href="/wiki/T%C5%8Dy%C5%8D_kanji" title="Tōyō kanji">it was changed</a> to <span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E6%B0%97" class="extiw" title="wikt:気">気</a></span>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The study of <i><a href="/wiki/Qi" title="Qi">ki</a></i> is an important component of aikido, and its study defies categorization as either "physical" or "mental" training, as it encompasses both. The <i><a href="/wiki/Kanji" title="Kanji">kanji</a></i> for <i>ki</i> normally is written as <span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">気</span>. It was written as <span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">氣</span> until the <a href="/wiki/T%C5%8Dy%C5%8D_kanji" title="Tōyō kanji">writing reforms after World War II</a>, and this older form still is seen on occasion.</p>
<p>The character for <i>ki</i> is used in everyday Japanese terms, such as "health" <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">元気</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>genki</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>, or "shyness" <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">内気</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>uchiki</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>. <i>Ki</i> has many meanings, including "ambience", "mind", "mood", and "intention", however, in traditional martial arts it is often used to refer to "life energy". <a href="/wiki/G%C5%8Dz%C5%8D_Shioda" class="mw-redirect" title="Gōzō Shioda">Gōzō Shioda</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Yoshinkan" title="Yoshinkan">Yoshinkan Aikido</a>, considered one of the "hard styles", largely follows Ueshiba's teachings from before World War II, and surmises that the secret to <i>ki</i> lies in timing and the application of the whole body's strength to a single point.<sup id="cite_ref-Shugyo_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shugyo-21">[21]</a></sup> In later years, Ueshiba's application of <i>ki</i> in aikido took on a softer, more gentle feel. This was his <a href="/wiki/Takemusu" class="mw-redirect" title="Takemusu">Takemusu</a> Aiki and many of his later students teach about <i>ki</i> from this perspective. <a href="/wiki/Koichi_Tohei" title="Koichi Tohei">Koichi Tohei</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Ki_Society" title="Ki Society">Ki Society</a> centers almost exclusively around the study of the <a href="/wiki/Empirical" class="mw-redirect" title="Empirical">empirical</a> (albeit subjective) experience of <i>ki</i> with students ranked separately in aikido techniques and <i>ki</i> development.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Training">Training</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aikido&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Training">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In aikido, as in virtually all Japanese <a href="/wiki/Martial_arts" title="Martial arts">martial arts</a>, there are both physical and mental aspects of training. The physical training in aikido is diverse, covering both general physical fitness and <a href="/wiki/Physical_exercise" title="Physical exercise">conditioning</a>, as well as specific techniques.<sup id="cite_ref-AFL_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AFL-23">[23]</a></sup> Because a substantial portion of any aikido curriculum consists of <a href="/wiki/Throw_(grappling)" title="Throw (grappling)">throws</a>, beginners learn how to safely fall or roll.<sup id="cite_ref-AFL_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AFL-23">[23]</a></sup> The specific techniques for attack include both strikes and grabs; the techniques for defense consist of throws and <a href="/wiki/Pinning_hold" class="mw-redirect" title="Pinning hold">pins</a>. After basic techniques are learned, students study freestyle defense against multiple opponents, and techniques with weapons.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fitness">Fitness</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aikido&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Fitness">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:A%C3%AFkido-shihoo_nage.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/A%C3%AFkido-shihoo_nage.jpg/200px-A%C3%AFkido-shihoo_nage.jpg" width="200" height="200" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/A%C3%AFkido-shihoo_nage.jpg/300px-A%C3%AFkido-shihoo_nage.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/A%C3%AFkido-shihoo_nage.jpg/400px-A%C3%AFkido-shihoo_nage.jpg 2x" data-file-width="945" data-file-height="945" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:A%C3%AFkido-shihoo_nage.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Uke_(martial_arts)" title="Uke (martial arts)">Ukemi</a> (<span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">受け身</span>) is very important for safe practice</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Physical training goals pursued in conjunction with aikido include controlled <a href="/wiki/Relaxation_technique" title="Relaxation technique">relaxation</a>, correct movement of joints such as hips and shoulders, <a href="/wiki/Flexibility_(anatomy)" title="Flexibility (anatomy)">flexibility</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Endurance" title="Endurance">endurance</a>, with less emphasis on <a href="/wiki/Strength_training" title="Strength training">strength training</a>. In aikido, pushing or extending movements are much more common than pulling or contracting movements. This distinction can be applied to general fitness goals for the aikido practitioner.<sup id="cite_ref-ADS_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ADS-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In aikido, specific muscles or muscle groups are not isolated and worked to improve tone, mass, or power. Aikido-related training emphasizes the use of coordinated whole-body movement and balance similar to <a href="/wiki/Yoga" title="Yoga">yoga</a> or <a href="/wiki/Pilates" title="Pilates">pilates</a>. For example, many dojos begin each class with warm-up exercises <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">準備体操</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>junbi taisō</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>, which may include <a href="/wiki/Stretching" title="Stretching">stretching</a> and <a href="/wiki/Uke_(martial_arts)" title="Uke (martial arts)">ukemi</a> (break falls).<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Roles_of_uke_and_tori">Roles of <i>uke</i> and <i>tori</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aikido&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Roles of uke and tori">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Aikido training is based primarily on two partners practicing pre-arranged forms (<i><a href="/wiki/Kata" title="Kata">kata</a></i>) rather than freestyle practice. The basic pattern is for the receiver of the technique (<i><a href="/wiki/Uke_(martial_arts)" title="Uke (martial arts)">uke</a></i>) to initiate an attack against the person who applies the technique—the <span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">取り</span> <i><a href="/wiki/Tori_(martial_arts)" title="Tori (martial arts)">tori</a></i>, or <i>shite</i> <span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">仕手</span> (depending on aikido style), also referred to as <span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">投げ</span> <i>nage</i> (when applying a throwing technique), who neutralises this attack with an aikido technique.<sup id="cite_ref-afl2_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-afl2-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Both halves of the technique, that of <i>uke</i> and that of <i>tori</i>, are considered essential to aikido training.<sup id="cite_ref-afl2_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-afl2-25">[25]</a></sup> Both are studying aikido principles of blending and adaptation. <i>Tori</i> learns to blend with and control attacking energy, while <i>uke</i> learns to become calm and flexible in the disadvantageous, off-balance positions in which <i>tori</i> places them. This "receiving" of the technique is called <i>ukemi</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-afl2_25-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-afl2-25">[25]</a></sup> <i>Uke</i> continuously seeks to regain balance and cover vulnerabilities (e.g., an exposed side), while <i>tori</i> uses position and timing to keep <i>uke</i> off-balance and vulnerable. In more advanced training, <i>uke</i> will sometimes apply reversal techniques <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">返し技</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>kaeshi-waza</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> to regain balance and pin or throw <i>tori</i>.</p>
<p><i>Ukemi</i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">受身</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> refers to the act of receiving a technique. Good <i>ukemi</i> involves attention to the technique, the partner and the immediate environment—it is an active rather than a passive receiving of aikido. The fall itself is part of aikido, and is a way for the practitioner to receive, safely, what would otherwise be a devastating strike or throw.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Initial_attacks">Initial attacks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aikido&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Initial attacks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Aikido techniques are usually a defense against an attack, so students must learn to deliver various types of attacks to be able to practice aikido with a partner. Although attacks are not studied as thoroughly as in striking-based arts, sincere attacks (a strong strike or an immobilizing grab) are needed to study correct and effective application of technique.<sup id="cite_ref-ADS_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ADS-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many of the strikes <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">打ち</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>uchi</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> of aikido resemble cuts from a <a href="/wiki/Sword" title="Sword">sword</a> or other grasped object, which indicate its origins in techniques intended for <a href="/wiki/Weapon" title="Weapon">armed</a> combat.<sup id="cite_ref-ADS_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ADS-2">[2]</a></sup> Other techniques, which explicitly appear to be punches (<i><a href="/wiki/Tsuki" title="Tsuki">tsuki</a></i>), are practiced as thrusts with a <a href="/wiki/Knife" title="Knife">knife</a> or sword. <a href="/wiki/Kick" title="Kick">Kicks</a> are generally reserved for upper-level variations; reasons cited include that falls from kicks are especially dangerous, and that kicks (high kicks in particular) were uncommon during the types of combat prevalent in feudal Japan. Some basic strikes include:</p>
<ul>
<li><b>Front-of-the-head strike</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">正面打ち</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>shōmen'uchi</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> a vertical <a href="/wiki/Knifehand_strike" title="Knifehand strike">knifehand strike</a> to the head. In training, this is usually directed at the forehead or the <a href="/wiki/Crown_(anatomy)" title="Crown (anatomy)">crown</a> for safety, but more dangerous versions of this attack target the bridge of the nose and the <a href="/wiki/Maxillary_sinus" title="Maxillary sinus">maxillary sinus</a>.</li>
<li><b>Side-of-the-head strike</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">横面打ち</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>yokomen'uchi</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> a diagonal knifehand strike to the side of the head or neck.</li>
<li><b>Chest thrust</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">胸突き</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>mune-tsuki</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> a <a href="/wiki/Punch_(strike)" class="mw-redirect" title="Punch (strike)">punch</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Torso" title="Torso">torso</a>. Specific targets include the <a href="/wiki/Chest" class="mw-redirect" title="Chest">chest</a>, <a href="/wiki/Abdomen" title="Abdomen">abdomen</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Solar_plexus" class="mw-redirect" title="Solar plexus">solar plexus</a>. Same as "middle-level thrust" <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">中段突き</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>chūdan-tsuki</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>, and "direct thrust" <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">直突き</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i><a href="/wiki/Choku-tsuki" class="mw-redirect" title="Choku-tsuki">choku-tsuki</a></i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>.</li>
<li><b>Face thrust</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">顔面突き</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>ganmen-tsuki</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> a punch to the <a href="/wiki/Face" title="Face">face</a>. Same as "upper-level thrust" <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">上段突き</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>jōdan-tsuki</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>.</li>
</ul>
<p>Beginners in particular often practice techniques from grabs, both because they are safer and because it is easier to feel the energy and lines of force of a hold than a strike. Some grabs are historically derived from being held while trying to draw a <a href="/wiki/Katana" title="Katana">weapon</a>; a technique could then be used to free oneself and immobilize or strike the attacker who is grabbing the defender.<sup id="cite_ref-ADS_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ADS-2">[2]</a></sup> The following are examples of some basic grabs:</p>
<ul>
<li><b>Single-hand grab</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">片手取り</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>katate-dori</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> one hand grabs one wrist.</li>
<li><b>Both-hands grab</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">諸手取り</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>morote-dori</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> both hands grab one wrist. Same as "single hand double-handed grab" <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">片手両手取り</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>katateryōte-dori</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span></li>
<li><b>Both-hands grab</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">両手取り</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>ryōte-dori</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> both hands grab both wrists. Same as "double single-handed grab" <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">両片手取り</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>ryōkatate-dori</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>.</li>
<li><b>Shoulder grab</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">肩取り</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>kata-dori</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> a shoulder grab. "Both-shoulders-grab" is <i>ryōkata-dori</i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">両肩取り</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>. It is sometimes combined with an overhead strike as <b>Shoulder grab face strike</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">肩取り面打ち</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>kata-dori men-uchi</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>.</li>
<li><b>Chest grab</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">胸取り</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>mune-dori or muna-dori</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> grabbing the (clothing of the) chest. Same as "collar grab" <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">襟取り</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>eri-dori</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Basic_techniques">Basic techniques</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aikido&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Basic techniques">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Aikido_techniques" title="Aikido techniques">Aikido techniques</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Aikido_ikkyo.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Aikido_ikkyo.svg/220px-Aikido_ikkyo.svg.png" width="220" height="100" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Aikido_ikkyo.svg/330px-Aikido_ikkyo.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Aikido_ikkyo.svg/440px-Aikido_ikkyo.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="613" data-file-height="280" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Aikido_ikkyo.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Diagram of <i>ikkyō</i>, or "first technique". <i>Yonkyō</i> has a similar mechanism of action, although the upper hand grips the forearm rather than the elbow.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The following are a sample of the basic or widely practiced throws and pins. Many of these techniques derive from Daitō-ryū Aiki-jūjutsu, but some others were invented by Morihei Ueshiba. The precise terminology for some may vary between organisations and styles, so what follows are the terms used by the Aikikai Foundation. Note that despite the names of the first five techniques listed, they are not universally taught in numeric order.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><b>First technique</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">一教 (<a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E6%95%99" class="extiw" title="wikt:教">教</a>)</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>ikkyō</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> a control using one hand on the elbow and one hand near the wrist which <a href="/wiki/Lever" title="Lever">leverages</a> <i>uke</i> to the ground.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> This grip applies pressure into the <a href="/wiki/Ulnar_nerve" title="Ulnar nerve">ulnar nerve</a> at the wrist.</li>
<li><b>Second technique</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">二教</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>nikyō</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> a <a href="/wiki/Kote_mawashi" class="mw-redirect" title="Kote mawashi">pronating wristlock</a> that torques the arm and applies painful nerve pressure. (There is an <a href="/wiki/Wristlock#Adductive_wristlock" title="Wristlock">adductive wristlock</a> or Z-lock in <i>ura</i> version.)</li>
<li><b>Third technique</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">三教</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>sankyō</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> a <a href="/wiki/Wristlock#Rotational_wristlock" title="Wristlock">rotational wristlock</a> that directs upward-spiraling tension throughout the arm, elbow and shoulder.</li>
<li><b>Fourth technique</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">四教</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>yonkyō</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> a shoulder control similar to <i>ikkyō</i>, but with both hands gripping the forearm. The knuckles (from the palm side) are applied to the recipient's <a href="/wiki/Radial_nerve" title="Radial nerve">radial nerve</a> against the <a href="/wiki/Periosteum" title="Periosteum">periosteum</a> of the forearm bone.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Fifth technique</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">五教</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>gokyō</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> visually similar to <i>ikkyō</i>, but with an inverted grip of the wrist, <a href="/wiki/Medial_rotation" class="mw-redirect" title="Medial rotation">medial rotation</a> of the arm and shoulder, and downward pressure on the elbow. Common in <a href="/wiki/Knife" title="Knife">knife</a> and other weapon take-aways.</li>
<li><b>Four-direction throw</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">四方投げ</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>shihōnage</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> the hand is folded back past the shoulder, locking the shoulder joint.</li>
<li><b>Forearm return</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">小手返し</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>kotegaeshi</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> a <a href="/wiki/Kote_gaeshi" class="mw-redirect" title="Kote gaeshi">supinating</a> wristlock-throw that stretches the <a href="/wiki/Extensor_digitorum" class="mw-redirect" title="Extensor digitorum">extensor digitorum</a>.</li>
<li><b>Breath throw</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">呼吸投げ</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>kokyūnage</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> a loosely used term for various types of mechanically unrelated techniques, although they generally do not use joint locks like other techniques.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Entering throw</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">入身投げ</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>iriminage</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> throws in which <i>tori</i> moves through the space occupied by <i>uke</i>. The classic form superficially resembles a "clothesline" technique.</li>
<li><b>Heaven-and-earth throw</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">天地投げ</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>tenchinage</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> beginning with <i>ryōte-dori</i>; moving forward, <i>tori</i> sweeps one hand low ("earth") and the other high ("heaven"), which unbalances <i>uke</i> so that he or she easily topples over.</li>
<li><b>Hip throw</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">腰投げ</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>koshinage</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> aikido's version of the <a href="/wiki/Throw_(grappling)#Hip_throws" title="Throw (grappling)">hip throw</a>. <i>Tori</i> drops their hips lower than those of <i>uke</i>, then flips <i>uke</i> over the resultant <a href="/wiki/Lever" title="Lever">fulcrum</a>.</li>
<li><b>Figure-ten throw</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">十字投げ</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>jūjinage</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> or <b>figure-ten entanglement</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">十字絡み</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>jūjigarami</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> a throw that locks the arms against each other (the <a href="/wiki/Kanji" title="Kanji">kanji</a> for "10" is a cross-shape: 十).<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Rotary throw</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">回転投げ</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>kaitennage</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> <i>Tori</i> sweeps the arm back until it locks the shoulder joint, then uses forward pressure to throw.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Implementations">Implementations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aikido&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Implementations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Aikido_ikkyo_omote_ura.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Aikido_ikkyo_omote_ura.png/220px-Aikido_ikkyo_omote_ura.png" width="220" height="104" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Aikido_ikkyo_omote_ura.png/330px-Aikido_ikkyo_omote_ura.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Aikido_ikkyo_omote_ura.png/440px-Aikido_ikkyo_omote_ura.png 2x" data-file-width="751" data-file-height="354" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Aikido_ikkyo_omote_ura.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Diagram showing two versions of the <i>ikkyō</i> technique: one moving forward (the <i>omote</i> version) and one moving backward (the <i>ura</i> version). See text for more details.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Aikido is more of a defensive martial art. It makes use of body movement (<i><a href="/wiki/Tai_sabaki" title="Tai sabaki">tai sabaki</a></i>) to blend with <i>uke</i>. For example, an "entering" (<i><a href="/wiki/Irimi" title="Irimi">irimi</a></i>) technique consists of movements inward towards <i>uke</i>, while a "turning" <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">転換</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>tenkan</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> technique uses a pivoting motion.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Additionally, an "inside" <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">内</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>uchi</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> technique takes place in front of <i>uke</i>, whereas an "outside" <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">外</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>soto</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> technique takes place to their side; a "front" <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">表</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>omote</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> technique is applied with motion to the front of <i>uke</i>, and a "rear" <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">裏</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>ura</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> version is applied with motion towards the rear of <i>uke</i>, usually by incorporating a turning or pivoting motion. Finally, most techniques can be performed while in a seated posture (<i><a href="/wiki/Seiza" title="Seiza">seiza</a></i>). Techniques where both <i>uke</i> and <i>tori</i> are standing are called <i>tachi-waza</i>, techniques where both start off in <i>seiza</i> are called <i>suwari-waza</i>, and techniques performed with <i>uke</i> standing and <i>tori</i> sitting are called <i>hanmi handachi</i> (<span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">半身半立</span>).<sup id="cite_ref-dynamic_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dynamic-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>Thus, from fewer than twenty basic techniques, there are thousands of possible implementations. For instance, <i>ikkyō</i> can be applied to an opponent moving forward with a strike (perhaps with an <i>ura</i> type of movement to redirect the incoming force), or to an opponent who has already struck and is now moving back to reestablish distance (perhaps an <i>omote-waza</i> version). Specific aikido <i>kata</i> are typically referred to with the formula "attack-technique(-modifier)". For instance, <i>katate-dori ikkyō</i> refers to any <i>ikkyō</i> technique executed when <i>uke</i> is holding one wrist. This could be further specified as <i>katate-dori ikkyō omote</i>, referring to any forward-moving <i>ikkyō</i> technique from that grab.</p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Atemi" title="Atemi">Atemi</a></i> (<span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">当て身</span>) are strikes (or <a href="/wiki/Feint" title="Feint">feints</a>) employed during an aikido technique. Some view <i>atemi</i> as attacks against "<a href="/wiki/Pressure_point" title="Pressure point">vital points</a>" meant to cause damage in and of themselves. For instance, <a href="/wiki/Gozo_Shioda" title="Gozo Shioda">Gōzō Shioda</a> described using <i>atemi</i> in a brawl to quickly down a gang's leader.<sup id="cite_ref-Shugyo_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shugyo-21">[21]</a></sup> Others consider <i>atemi</i>, especially to the face, to be methods of distraction meant to enable other techniques. A strike, whether or not it is blocked, can startle the target and break their concentration. The target may become unbalanced in attempting to avoid the blow, for example by jerking the head back, which may allow for an easier throw.<sup id="cite_ref-dynamic_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dynamic-33">[33]</a></sup> Many sayings about <i>atemi</i> are attributed to Morihei Ueshiba, who considered them an essential element of technique.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Weapons">Weapons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aikido&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Weapons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:PRehse002-cropped.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/8/88/PRehse002-cropped.jpg/220px-PRehse002-cropped.jpg" width="220" height="206" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/8/88/PRehse002-cropped.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="254" data-file-height="238" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:PRehse002-cropped.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Disarming an attacker using a "sword taking" <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">太刀取り</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>tachi-dori</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> technique.</div>

<p>Weapons training in aikido traditionally includes the short staff (<i><a href="/wiki/J%C5%8D" title="Jō">jō</a></i>), wooden sword (<i><a href="/wiki/Bokken" title="Bokken">bokken</a></i>), and knife (<i><a href="/wiki/Tant%C5%8D" title="Tantō">tantō</a></i>).<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Some schools incorporate firearm-disarming techniques. Both weapon-taking and weapon-retention are taught. Some schools, such as the <a href="/wiki/Iwama_style" title="Iwama style">Iwama style</a> of <a href="/wiki/Morihiro_Saito" title="Morihiro Saito">Morihiro Saito</a>, usually spend substantial time with <i>bokken</i> and <i>jō</i>, practised under the names <i><a href="/wiki/Aiki-ken" title="Aiki-ken">aiki-ken</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Aiki-j%C5%8D" title="Aiki-jō">aiki-jō</a></i>, respectively.</p>
<p>The founder developed many of the empty-handed techniques from traditional sword and spear movements. Consequently, the practice of the weapons arts gives insight into the origin of techniques and movements, and reinforces the concepts of distance, timing, foot movement, presence and connectedness with one's training partner(s).<sup id="cite_ref-SOS_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SOS-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Multiple_attackers_and_randori">Multiple attackers and <i>randori</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aikido&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Multiple attackers and randori">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>One feature of aikido is training to defend against multiple attackers, often called <i>taninzudori</i>, or <i>taninzugake</i>. Freestyle practice with multiple attackers, called <i><a href="/wiki/Randori" title="Randori">randori</a></i> (<span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">乱取</span>) or <i>jiyūwaza</i> (<span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">自由技</span>), is a key part of most curricula and is required for the higher level ranks.<sup id="cite_ref-best_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-best-37">[37]</a></sup> <i>Randori</i> exercises a person's ability to intuitively perform techniques in an unstructured environment.<sup id="cite_ref-best_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-best-37">[37]</a></sup> Strategic choice of techniques, based on how they reposition the student relative to other attackers, is important in <i>randori</i> training. For instance, an <i>ura</i> technique might be used to neutralise the current attacker while turning to face attackers approaching from behind.<sup id="cite_ref-ADS_2-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ADS-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Shodokan_Aikido" title="Shodokan Aikido">Shodokan Aikido</a>, <i>randori</i> differs in that it is not performed with multiple persons with defined roles of defender and attacker, but between two people, where both participants attack, defend, and counter at will. In this respect it resembles judo <i>randori</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-ACE_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ACE-19">[19]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Injuries">Injuries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aikido&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Injuries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In applying a technique during training, it is the responsibility of <i>tori</i> to prevent injury to <i>uke</i> by employing a speed and force of application that is commensurate with their partner's proficiency in <i>ukemi</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-afl2_25-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-afl2-25">[25]</a></sup> Injuries (especially those to the joints), when they do occur in aikido, are often the result of <i>tori</i> misjudging the ability of <i>uke</i> to receive the throw or pin.<sup id="cite_ref-Aikinjuries_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Aikinjuries-39">[39]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Aikinjuries2_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Aikinjuries2-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>A study of injuries in the martial arts showed that the type of injuries varied considerably from one art to the other.<sup id="cite_ref-injurycomparison_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-injurycomparison-41">[41]</a></sup> Soft tissue injuries are one of the most common types of injuries found within aikido,<sup id="cite_ref-injurycomparison_41-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-injurycomparison-41">[41]</a></sup> as well as joint strain and stubbed fingers and toes.<sup id="cite_ref-Aikinjuries2_40-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Aikinjuries2-40">[40]</a></sup> Several deaths from head-and-neck injuries, caused by aggressive <i>shihōnage</i> in a <a href="/wiki/Senpai_and_k%C5%8Dhai" title="Senpai and kōhai">senpai/kōhai</a> hazing context, have been reported.<sup id="cite_ref-Aikinjuries_39-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Aikinjuries-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mental_training">Mental training</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aikido&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Mental training">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Aikido training is mental as well as physical, emphasizing the ability to relax the mind and body even under the stress of dangerous situations.<sup id="cite_ref-ZENMA_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ZENMA-42">[42]</a></sup> This is necessary to enable the practitioner to perform the bold enter-and-blend movements that underlie aikido techniques, wherein an attack is met with confidence and directness.<sup id="cite_ref-AFL_1-9_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AFL_1-9-43">[43]</a></sup> Morihei Ueshiba once remarked that one "must be willing to receive 99% of an opponent's attack and stare death in the face" in order to execute techniques without hesitation.<sup id="cite_ref-AOP_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AOP-44">[44]</a></sup> As a martial art concerned not only with fighting proficiency but with the betterment of daily life, this mental aspect is of key importance to aikido practitioners.<sup id="cite_ref-NewWarrior_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NewWarrior-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uniforms_and_ranking">Uniforms and ranking</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aikido&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Uniforms and ranking">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:232px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Folding_hakama.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Folding_hakama.jpg/230px-Folding_hakama.jpg" width="230" height="153" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Folding_hakama.jpg/345px-Folding_hakama.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Folding_hakama.jpg/460px-Folding_hakama.jpg 2x" data-file-width="500" data-file-height="332" /></a>

<p>Aikido practitioners (commonly called <i>aikidōka</i> outside Japan) generally progress by promotion through a series of "grades" (<i><a href="/wiki/Ky%C5%AB" title="Kyū">kyū</a></i>), followed by a series of "degrees" (<i><a href="/wiki/Dan_(rank)" title="Dan (rank)">dan</a></i>), pursuant to formal testing procedures. Some aikido organizations use belts to distinguish practitioners' grades, often simply white and <a href="/wiki/Black_belt_(martial_arts)" title="Black belt (martial arts)">black belts</a> to distinguish <i>kyu</i> and <i>dan</i> grades, though some use various belt colors. Testing requirements vary, so a particular rank in one organization is not comparable or interchangeable with the rank of another.<sup id="cite_ref-ADS_2-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ADS-2">[2]</a></sup> Some dojos do not allow students to take the test to obtain a <i>dan</i> rank unless they are 16 or older.</p>

<p>The uniform worn for practicing aikido (<i><a href="/wiki/Aikidogi" class="mw-redirect" title="Aikidogi">aikidōgi</a></i>) is similar to the training uniform (<i><a href="/wiki/Keikogi" title="Keikogi">keikogi</a></i>) used in most other modern martial arts; simple trousers and a wraparound jacket, usually white. Both thick ("<a href="/wiki/Judo" title="Judo">judo</a>-style"), and thin ("<a href="/wiki/Karate" title="Karate">karate</a>-style") cotton tops are used.<sup id="cite_ref-ADS_2-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ADS-2">[2]</a></sup> Aikido-specific tops are available with shorter sleeves which reach to just below the elbow.</p>
<p>Most aikido systems add a pair of wide pleated black or <a href="/wiki/Indigo" title="Indigo">indigo</a> trousers called a <i><a href="/wiki/Hakama" title="Hakama">hakama</a></i> (used also in <a href="/wiki/Kendo" title="Kendo">kendo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iaido" title="Iaido">iaido</a>). In many schools, its use is reserved for practitioners with (<i>dan</i>) ranks or for instructors, while others allow all practitioners to wear a <i>hakama</i> regardless of rank.<sup id="cite_ref-ADS_2-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ADS-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticisms">Criticisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aikido&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Criticisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The most common criticism of aikido is that it suffers from a lack of realism in training. The attacks initiated by <i>uke</i> (and which <i>tori</i> must defend against) have been criticized as being "weak", "sloppy", and "little more than caricatures of an attack".<sup id="cite_ref-Pranin-aikido_today_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pranin-aikido_today-46">[46]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> Weak attacks from <i>uke</i> allow for a conditioned response from <i>tori</i>, and result in underdevelopment of the skills needed for the safe and effective practice of both partners.<sup id="cite_ref-Pranin-aikido_today_46-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pranin-aikido_today-46">[46]</a></sup> To counteract this, some styles allow students to become less compliant over time but, in keeping with the core philosophies, this is after having demonstrated proficiency in being able to protect themselves and their training partners. <a href="/wiki/Shodokan_Aikido" title="Shodokan Aikido">Shodokan Aikido</a> addresses the issue by practising in a competitive format.<sup id="cite_ref-ACE_19-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ACE-19">[19]</a></sup> Such adaptations are debated between styles, with some maintaining that there is no need to adjust their methods because either the criticisms are unjustified, or that they are not training for self-defense or combat effectiveness, but spiritual, fitness or other reasons.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another criticism pertains to the shift in training focus after the end of Ueshiba's seclusion in Iwama from 1942 to the mid-1950s, as he increasingly emphasized the spiritual and philosophical aspects of aikido. As a result, strikes to vital points by <i>tori</i>, entering (<i>irimi</i>) and initiation of techniques by <i>tori</i>, the distinction between <i>omote</i> (front side) and <i>ura</i> (back side) techniques, and the use of weapons, were all de-emphasized or eliminated from practice. Some Aikido practitioners feel that lack of training in these areas leads to an overall loss of effectiveness.<sup id="cite_ref-Pranin-status_quo_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pranin-status_quo-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>Conversely, some styles of aikido receive criticism for not placing enough importance on the spiritual practices emphasized by Ueshiba. According to Minoru Shibata of Aikido Journal, "O-Sensei's aikido was not a continuation and extension of the old and has a distinct discontinuity with past martial and philosophical concepts."<sup id="cite_ref-Shibata-Dilemma_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shibata-Dilemma-50">[50]</a></sup> That is, that aikido practitioners who focus on aikido's roots in traditional <i><a href="/wiki/Jujutsu" title="Jujutsu">jujutsu</a></i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Kenjutsu" title="Kenjutsu">kenjutsu</a></i> are diverging from what Ueshiba taught. Such critics urge practitioners to embrace the assertion that "[Ueshiba's] transcendence to the spiritual and universal reality were the fundamentals  [<i><a href="/wiki/Sic" title="Sic">sic</a></i>] of the paradigm that he demonstrated."<sup id="cite_ref-Shibata-Dilemma_50-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shibata-Dilemma-50">[50]</a></sup></p>

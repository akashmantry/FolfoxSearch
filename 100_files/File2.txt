<p><b>Çanakkale</b> (pronounced <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Turkic_dialects" title="Help:IPA for Turkic dialects">[tʃaˈnakkaˌle]</a></span>; from <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Turkish_language" title="Ottoman Turkish language">Ottoman Turkish</a> <span lang="ota" dir="rtl">چینک قلعه</span>‎, literally "pot fortress") is a city and seaport in <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>, in <a href="/wiki/%C3%87anakkale_Province" title="Çanakkale Province">Çanakkale Province</a>, on the southern (Asian) coast of the <a href="/wiki/Dardanelles" title="Dardanelles">Dardanelles</a> at their narrowest point. The population of the town is 186,116 (2014 estimate).<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The current mayor is Ülgür Gökhan (<a href="/wiki/Republican_People%27s_Party_(Turkey)" title="Republican People's Party (Turkey)">CHP</a>).</p>
<p>Çanakkale Province, like <a href="/wiki/Istanbul_Province" title="Istanbul Province">Istanbul Province</a>, has territory in both <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>. Ferries cross there to the northern (European) side of the strait.</p>
<p>Çanakkale is the finishing point every year for an organised swim across the Hellespont from <a href="/wiki/Eceabat" title="Eceabat">Eceabat</a>. This event emulates the swim in 1810 by <a href="/wiki/Lord_Byron" title="Lord Byron">Lord Byron</a>, who was himself emulating the legendary swim by Leander in the story of <a href="/wiki/Hero_and_Leander" title="Hero and Leander">Hero and Leander</a>.</p>
<p>The city is the nearest major town to the site of ancient <a href="/wiki/Troy" title="Troy">Troy</a>. The "wooden horse" from the 2004 movie <i><a href="/wiki/Troy_(movie)" class="mw-redirect" title="Troy (movie)">Troy</a></i> is exhibited on the seafront. Çanakkale is the second city to be <a href="/w/index.php?title=Transcontinental_city&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Transcontinental city (page does not exist)">situated on two continents</a> after <a href="/wiki/Istanbul" title="Istanbul">Istanbul</a>, but Çanakkale is closer to mid-division than Istanbul.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Name">Name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%87anakkale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Çanakkale is the name for a site earlier known as Kale-I-Sultaniye, which was adopted as the official term for the town in 1890, though current a century earlier.<sup id="cite_ref-Cook_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cook-4">[4]</a></sup> Çanakkale was an <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman</a> fortress called <i>Kale-i Sultaniye</i> (<a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Turkish_language" title="Ottoman Turkish language">Ottoman Turkish</a>: <span lang="ota-Arab" dir="rtl">قلعة سلطانيه</span>‎) or <i>Sultaniye kalesi</i> (Fortress of the Sultan). From the late 17th century it also became known for its fine-glazed pottery, <a href="/wiki/%C3%87anakkale_ceramics" title="Çanakkale ceramics">Çanakkale ceramics</a>, compared by one traveler to <a href="/wiki/Delftware" title="Delftware">Delftware</a> hence the later name <i>Çanak kalesi</i> 'pot fortress'. The <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Greek Byzantine</a> name for Çanakkale چینک قله was Δαρδανέλλια, <i>Dardanellia</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> from which the <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> name <a href="/wiki/Dardanelles" title="Dardanelles">Dardanelles</a> is derived.</p>
<p>As of 1920, the <a href="/wiki/British_people" title="British people">British</a> began to call Çanakkale, <b>Chanak</b> and <b>Kale Sultanie</b> in their reporting.<sup id="cite_ref-Prothero76_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Prothero76-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legends">Legends</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%87anakkale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Legends">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ancient <a href="/wiki/Abydos,_Hellespont" class="mw-redirect" title="Abydos, Hellespont">Abydos</a>, where the story of <a href="/wiki/Hero_and_Leander" title="Hero and Leander">Hero and Leander</a> takes place, is to the north of Çanakkale.</p>

<p>The first inhabitants of the area, which hosted many civilizations, lived on the <a href="/wiki/Biga,_%C3%87anakkale" title="Biga, Çanakkale">Biga</a> Peninsula in the Last <a href="/wiki/Chalcolithic" title="Chalcolithic">Chalcolithic</a> Age c. 6000 years ago. However, very little is known about the identity and lifestyle of these early settlers. According to some excavations and research, the earliest settlements in the region were established at <a href="/wiki/Kumtepe" title="Kumtepe">Kumtepe</a>. It is supposed that <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kumkale&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Kumkale (page does not exist)">Kumkale</a> was established in 4000 BC and <a href="/wiki/Troy" title="Troy">Troy</a> between 3500–3000 BC.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Aeolians" title="Aeolians">Aeolian Greeks</a> settled on the land in the 8th century BC and established trade colonies in the region called <a href="/wiki/Aeolis" title="Aeolis">Aeolis</a>. The region came under the control of the <a href="/wiki/Lydia" title="Lydia">Lydians</a> in the 7th century BC and under the control of the <a href="/wiki/Persia" class="mw-redirect" title="Persia">Persians</a> in the 6th century BC. Aeolis went under the control of the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Macedonian_army" title="Ancient Macedonian army">Ancient Macedonian army</a> as <a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">Alexander the Great</a> defeated the Persians by the <a href="/wiki/Biga_%C3%87ay%C4%B1" title="Biga Çayı">Granicus River</a> of the region in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Granicus" title="Battle of the Granicus">Battle of the Granicus</a> on his way to <a href="/wiki/Asia_Minor" class="mw-redirect" title="Asia Minor">Asia</a>. The region came under the reign of the Kingdom of <a href="/wiki/Pergamon" title="Pergamon">Pergamon</a> in the 2nd century BC.</p>
<p>Çanakkale itself was built as a fortress in 1462 by <a href="/wiki/Mehmed_the_Conqueror" title="Mehmed the Conqueror">Mehmed 11 Fatih</a>, who gave it the name Kale-I Sultaniye, since one of the Sultan's sons had collaborated in its construction.<sup id="cite_ref-Cook_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cook-4">[4]</a></sup> Sited on a bay at the narrowest point of the strait, it, together with another nearby fort, Kilid Bahr, provided an excellent position for controlling traffic through the Dardanelles. The two forts were quickly called in the travel literature "The Castles", and a town developed to the north-east, settled by Armenian refugees and Spanish Jews.</p>
<p>From the Renaissance period onwards, after their <a href="/wiki/Alhambra_Decree" title="Alhambra Decree">expulsion from Spain</a>, Jewish refugees settled in Çanakkale and formed a sizeable community which thrived by supplying Mediterranean shipping in the region with provisions and acting as consular agents for many European nations. Down to the late 19th century they retained Spanish as a mother-tongue. Some 1,805 Jews were registered there in 1890, out of a population of 10,862, the rest being Muslims (3,551), Orthodox Greeks (2,577), Armenians (956) and assorted foreigners (2,173).,<sup id="cite_ref-Cook_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cook-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The western part of the Biga Peninsula where ancient Troy is situated was called <a href="/wiki/Troad" title="Troad">Troas</a>. <a href="/wiki/Alexandria_Troas" title="Alexandria Troas">Alexandria Troas</a>, an important settlement of the region, was a free trade port and a rich trade center during <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman</a> times. Later in the 2nd century AD, the region was attacked by <a href="/wiki/Goths" title="Goths">Goths</a> from <a href="/wiki/Thrace" title="Thrace">Thrace</a>. During the 7th and 8th centuries, in order to attack <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a> the <a href="/wiki/Arab" class="mw-redirect" title="Arab">Arabs</a> passed the strait a few times and came up to <a href="/wiki/Sestos" title="Sestos">Sestos</a>. At the beginning of the 14th century the <a href="/wiki/Karasids" title="Karasids">Karasids</a> dominated the Anatolian part of the strait. During the first half of that century <a href="/w/index.php?title=Demirhan_Bey&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Demirhan Bey (page does not exist)">Demirhan Bey</a> from Karasids attempted to dominate the region. The <a href="/wiki/Ottomans" class="mw-redirect" title="Ottomans">Ottomans</a> gained control of <a href="/wiki/Gallipoli" title="Gallipoli">Gallipoli</a> in 1367.</p>
<p>In 1915, during the <a href="/wiki/First_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="First World War">First World War</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_of_Great_Britain_and_Ireland" title="United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland">British Empire</a> and <a href="/wiki/French_Third_Republic" title="French Third Republic">France</a> attempted to secure the waterway through the Straits and ultimately capture Constantinople. Known as The <a href="/wiki/Gallipoli_Campaign" title="Gallipoli Campaign">Gallipoli Campaign</a>, or the Dardanelles Campaign, in Turkey it is referred to as the Battle of Çanakkale (<a href="/wiki/Turkish_language" title="Turkish language">Turkish</a>: <span lang="tr"><i>Çanakkale Savaşı</i></span>), during March 1915 when the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> failed to force the Dardanelles and suffered severe losses. During a series of operations, <a href="/wiki/HMS_Triumph_(1903)" title="HMS Triumph (1903)"><i>HMS Triumph</i></a>, <a href="/wiki/HMS_Ocean_(1898)" title="HMS Ocean (1898)"><i>HMS Ocean</i></a>, <a href="/wiki/HMS_Goliath_(1898)" title="HMS Goliath (1898)"><i>HMS Goliath</i></a>, <a href="/wiki/HMS_Irresistible_(1898)" title="HMS Irresistible (1898)"><i>HMS Irresistible</i></a> and the French battleship <i><a href="/wiki/French_battleship_Bouvet" title="French battleship Bouvet">Bouvet</a></i> were all sunk. The French submarine Q84 <i><a href="/wiki/French_submarine_Joule_(Q84)" class="mw-redirect" title="French submarine Joule (Q84)">Joule</a></i> and the Australian submarine <i><a href="/wiki/HMAS_AE2" title="HMAS AE2">AE2</a></i> were also destroyed and several other important ships were crippled too. Most of the damage was inflicted by mines, though a German U-Boat and Turkish small craft contributed too.</p>
<p>As of 1920, the city was estimated to have a population of approximately 22,000. An active <a href="/wiki/Port_city" class="mw-redirect" title="Port city">port city</a>, it was a stopping point for vessels traveling through the strait, as it had been in the ancient past. It was described as lacking quality accommodations and resources for those passing through by the British who visited the region. Exported goods from the city included <a href="/wiki/Wine" title="Wine">wine</a>, hides, <a href="/wiki/Pottery" title="Pottery">pottery</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ceramic_tile" class="mw-redirect" title="Ceramic tile">ceramic tiles</a> and grain.<sup id="cite_ref-Prothero76_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Prothero76-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%87anakkale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The service of <a href="/wiki/Education" title="Education">education</a> throughout the city is above the country average. There are 13 high schools and a college within the boundaries of the city. <a href="/wiki/%C3%87anakkale_Onsekiz_Mart_University" title="Çanakkale Onsekiz Mart University">Çanakkale Onsekiz Mart University</a> has 12 faculties, 4 institutes, 9 4-year colleges, 14 vocational schools and serves more than 37,500 students. 30 percent of the city population are college graduates.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transportation">Transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%87anakkale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Transportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Çanakkale has one airport, which is 3&#160;km from the city centre, serving since 1995. <a href="/wiki/Anadolu_Jet" class="mw-redirect" title="Anadolu Jet">Anadolu Jet</a>, a trademark of <a href="/wiki/Turkish_Airlines" title="Turkish Airlines">Turkish Airlines</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Borajet" title="Borajet">Borajet</a> has flights from Istanbul and <a href="/wiki/Ankara" title="Ankara">Ankara</a> seven days a week.</p>
<p>Sea transportation is vital for the city since it is located on both sides, on Asian and European continents just like Istanbul.</p>
<p>Çanakkale is linked to north, east, and south by well-paved highways numbered E87/E90/<a href="/wiki/State_road_D550_(Turkey)" title="State road D550 (Turkey)"><img alt="D-550" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/D_550.png/49px-D_550.png" width="49" height="13" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/D_550.png/75px-D_550.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/D_550.png/97px-D_550.png 2x" data-file-width="1099" data-file-height="296" /></a> , E90/D200, and E87/D550 respectively. There are buses from Istanbul and <a href="/wiki/Izmir" class="mw-redirect" title="Izmir">Izmir</a> at any time, day or night. It takes five and a half to six hours to get from Istanbul to Çanakkale, and about the same time from Izmir.</p>

<p>North of Çanakkale at <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=%C3%87anakkale&amp;params=40_10_17.66_N_26_24_34.34_E_type:landmark&amp;title=TBG+transmitter"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">40°10′17.66″N</span> <span class="longitude">26°24′34.34″E</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="vcard"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">40.1715722°N 26.4095389°E</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">40.1715722; 26.4095389</span></span><span style="display:none">﻿ (<span class="fn org">TBG transmitter</span>)</span></span></span></a></span>, there is a guyed mast used for the maritime LF-transmitter TBG, working on 53.4&#160;kHz.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%87anakkale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Çanakkale has a <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_climate" title="Mediterranean climate">mediterranean climate</a> with hot and dry summers and cool and rainy winters. Snow falls ordinarily every winter.</p>
